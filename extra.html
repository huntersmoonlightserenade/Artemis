Modern Religion

She's still a very relevant figure for Hellenic polytheist community even to this day, even if her role and meaning has changed throughout the years.

Her role

She’s a goddess of hunting, which was seen as an activity for survival in ancient times. Nowadays hunting is mostly for recreation, which means Artemis’s roles needed to change with the times. She's seen today as a goddess of nature, the moon, chastity, protector of women, young girls and a companion during childbirth.

A strong part of her associations are the wild beasts of nature, bears, wolves, boars and calm creatures such as deer, of course.

In the cultural aspect, she’s a goddess of dancing and music, similar to her twin brother Apollon.

Now, as a Theoi, she’s a force of nature, which means good things and bad things in her domain, like any other god. She’s the goddess of plagues, disease and sudden death.

In the opinion of this writer, Artemis is a goddess of strength, she teaches you to trust yourself and see the beauty of nature in everything, as well as finding control and strength over your own body. She invites you to dance freely, wildly and by choice.

Epithets

  • Aristo“the best” - A surname used to worship her in Athens
  • Aristoboule “Wisest in council” - Had a temple in Athens
  • Agrotera “in the fields”
  • Delia “from Delos” - Surname shared with Apollon
  • Ephesia - In Ephesus stand her biggest temple, revered as a goddess of fertility
  • Iokheaira “who delights in arrows”
  • Khryselakatos “with golden arrows”
  • Letogeneia “Daughter of Leto”
  • Melissa - A surname as a goddess of the moon, protector of women in childbed
  • Orthia - A surname used to worship her in Sparta
  • Parthenia “the maiden”
  • Phíli “Beloved”
  • Phoebe “bright” or “shining” - Surname as a goddess of the moon, shared with Phoebus Apollon as a sun god
  • Phosphorus “light bringer” - Also related to light, referring to her role as a moon goddess
  • Theroskopos “hunter of wild beasts”

Associations

a. Representations

The most recognizable symbols of Artemis are the bow and arrow, but she was represented sometimes with hunting spears, torches, and even lyres.

She was often represented with a girl's knee high dress or a chiton, a full length robe. Also seen wearing a cloak and multiple different headgear like crowns, bonnets, headbands and animal-pelt caps. All these mostly in ancient pottery and Greek art, this changed with renaissance pieces and her mixture with Diana, her Roman counterpart.

b. Sacred animals and plants

Her sacred animal is the deer, she's often represented hunting a deer or with a deer pelt over her shoulders. She's associated with bears and other wild animals of the forest, along with some birds like herons, partridges, quails and guinea-fowl.

Hymns

Hymns were and still are used as a form of prayer and a way to honor the Theoi (the gods) with our words, here are hymns written for our Letogeneia (daughter of Leto)

Orphic Hymn 36 To Artemis

Hear me, Daughter of Leto, Celebrated, Noble, Bacchian Queen, rejoicing in Your silver arrows, Torch-bearing Goddess Dictunna, who presides over births and relieves the pain of labor, Divine Maiden, who glories in the Sylvan hunt, Swift and fierce, no one is Your equal with the bow.

Wandering by night, reveling in the flowering meadows, Brave and beautiful Nurturer of mortals, Eternal and Earthly, Bane of fell monsters, Dweller in the hills whom the woods and dogs delight, You hunt the stag and give the Earth a store of bounteous fruit to bear.

Oh Universal Queen, You flourish in endless youth, Wield the Cydonian Power, Dread Guardian Goddess, With benevolence come to these mystic rites, Send us gentle Peace and Health, and drive disease and care far from us.

Translation by A. Athanassakis (sourced from Hellenion)

Homeric Hymn 27 to Artemis

I sing of Artemis with shafts are of gold (khryselakatos), strong-voiced (keladeine), the revered virgin (parthenon aidoin), dear-shooting (elaphebolos), delighter in arrows (iokheaira), own sister to Apollon of the golden sword (khrysaor).

Over the shadowy hills and windy peaks she draws her golden bow, rejoicing in the chase, and sends out grievous shafts.

The tops of the high mountains tremble and the tangled wood echoes awesomely with the outcry of beasts: earthquakes and the sea also where fishes shoal.

But the goddess with a bold heart turns every way destroying the race of wild beasts: and when she is satisfied and has cheered her heart, then the huntress who delights in arrows (theroskopos iokheaira) slackens her supple bow and goes to the great house of her dear brother Phoibos Apollon, to the rich land of Delphoi, there to order the lovely dance of the Mousai (Muses) and Kharites (Charites, Graces).

There she hangs up her curved bow and her arrows, and heads and leads the dances, gracefully arrayed, while all they utter their heavenly voice, singing how neat-ankled Leto bare children supreme among the immortals both in thought and deed. Hail to you, children of Zeus and rich-haired Leto! And now I will remember you and another song also.

Translation by Evelyn-White (sourced from Hellenion)
